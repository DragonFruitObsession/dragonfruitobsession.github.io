Propagation

This page covers how dragon fruit plants are propagated: from cuttings, seeds, grafts, and tissue culture. For other aspects of growing dragon fruit, such as basic care, trellising, and pollination, see the drop-down menu above.

Cuttings

Dragon fruit plants grow best from cuttings. Look for thick, healthy, mature stems and let them cure in the shade for a week to reduce the chance of rot. I like to chop at the node (points between two branches) if possible as this part of the plant is less prone to rotting. However, if this is not possible or I want to chop longer branches into multiple cuttings, I like to do a “flesh to stem cut” (see how) which effectively creates a node. Do this immediately and let cure for a week with your other cuttings. For beginners, it is helpful to take note of which way the branch was growing in relation to the base of the plant, imagining the flow of nutrients and water delivered to this branch (not orientation alone), as cuttings will only root if planted in this direction. As a general rule, plant with the spikes facing upwards. Cuttings should be planted a maximum of a few centimetres deep in a mixture of compost and free draining soil, to minimise risk of rot. Some growers, especially in very wet areas, simply place the cuttings on top of the soil to root, further reducing the chance of rot. I like to establish my cuttings in small pots (see picture) before planting out. Water well, and don’t let the soil dry out completely before watering again. From here, train any new growth up a stake (I tie the new growth to a bamboo stick for support), and wait for the plant to be around a metre high before planting into its final location. While this is not essential, I find it gives the cuttings a head start, and less nutrients are wasted as the plant can already uptake them in its new location.

I like to root dragon fruit cuttings in spring or summer if I can, as the roots form faster and there is less chance of rot with the reduced moisture. However, sometimes the need does come during winter (such as the loss of branches in a storm), in which case just use the same method if you live in a frost-free area, and wait a little longer. If you do get frost, there are a few things you can do to increase your chances of success. Consider applying rooting hormone to the soil end of the cuttings and placing them in a bed of coconut coir over a heat mat to stimulate growth. If you really want to get serious, purchase a grow light and do this all indoors (perhaps in a nifty little greenhouse), ready to plant out in spring. I've also seen people simply leave their cuttings in a bucket of water to develop roots, which might be worth a try in any season, or if you have cuttings you don't know what to do with.

Seedlings

I would advise against growing dragon fruits from seeds in most cases, as they grow incredibly slowly, and it may be upwards of five years before you get any fruit. Further, not all varieties are true to type, so after all that waiting you may end up with something not worth keeping. Of course, there is the possibility of ending up with something better than you started with, which is precisely the reason seedlings are used for dragon fruit hybridisation. This is the main reason dragon fruit enthusiasts grow seedlings. One variety is cross-pollinated by another, and the seeds from the best fruit are planted out in large numbers. The weak seedlings are pruned away, leaving only the strongest seedlings which can then be grown out in full or grafted (more on that below), and the fruit evaluated. This is a great way to get varieties suited to your climate, as the genetic variance introduced by cross-pollination (seeds will be genetically different) means the most adept plants will be the strongest, and the weaker plants will be removed. The same concept can be applied to varieties true to type (typically self-pollinating varieties), in which the seeds will be more genetically similar to their parents, but slight alterations could give more heat-tolerant plants for example. Of course, some people enjoy the challenge of growing fruit trees (or cacti, in this case) from seeds, in which case sow as you would vegetables.

Grafting

For slow-growing varieties, such as Yellow Colombiana, it can be beneficial to graft onto a strong rootstock such as Isis Gold or Vietnam White, as its own root system is not very strong. Grafting is also a great way to get many plants from a small amount of biomass, as it can work for very small pieces of material (see V-cut grafting). Some people have even successfully grafted pieces of stem left on store bought dragon fruit, allowing them to clone commercial varieties such as Palora (see clone grafting). Another use for grafting is to speed up the growth of seedlings, especially in the development of new varieties. I won’t go into too much detail on the methods for grafting, as there is an entire YouTube channel dedicated to it. I’ll list some of his most useful grafting tutorials below:

  • Horizontal graft (simplest graft you can do)
  • V-cut graft (when you have next to no material or want your material to spread very far)
  • Clone graft (how to clone the plant from shop fruits)
  • Seedling graft (great for hybridisation)
One thing I will say is that grafting cacti is much easier than grafting fruit trees, so don't be afraid of giving it a go! Bigger grafts tend to be easier as they are less prone to drying out. And don't think you need any expensive tools either. A knife sterilised with boiling water and masking tape to fasten will do the job just fine.

Tissue Culture

There is (at least) one more method of propagating dragon fruit out there, known as tissue culture, however it can only be done with specialised equipment. Tissue cultured plants are grown from tiny pieces of material (known as the explant), sometimes even a single cell, in a carefully controlled medium filled with nutrients and various growth stimulants. I don't know a great deal about the whole process, and it's not particularly relevant to the average home gardener (apart from when you see it at the nursery), so I'll leave the reader to do their own research if they are interested. I do know that tissue cultured plants from varieties such as Edgar’s Baby can be purchased from nurseries in the US, so this is not as futuristic as you might think. In any case, it is a great way to mass-produce rare plants.
